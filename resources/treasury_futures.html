Treasury Futures

Treasury futures are standardized contracts traded on futures exchanges with Treasury securities as underlying assets. By using Treasury futures, banks can hedge against potential adverse movements in interest rates, thereby stabilizing their financial position and ensuring more predictable cash flows.

Contract Specifications

Treasury futures have a quarterly cycle with expiration months in March, June, September, and December. For each type of Treasury futures, there are always four contracts trading simultaneously, each corresponding to one of the quarterly expiration months.

As an example, suppose it is January 2024, the trading contracts are for March 2024, June 2024, September 2024, and December 2024. When the March 2024 contract approaches expiration, a new March 2025 contract will begin trading.

This means the maturity of each contract is typically less than a year. As one contract nears expiration, positions will need to be rolled into the next available contract to maintain continuous hedging against interest rate movements.
